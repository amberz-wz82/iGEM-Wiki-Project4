Our Current Work

This year, Cornell iGEM has developed a cancer bacteriotherapy system housed within engineered E. Coli cells. Paired with a patient’s main therapy, our system aims to identify the physical location of breast cancer metastasis and increase tumor cell elimination capabilities. Our genetically engineered bacteria’s genome contains a Holin/Anti-Holin kill switch that allows the bacteria to survive and reproduce in high lactate tumor environments while forcing bacteria to commit apoptosis in low level lactate concentrations. This ensures that our bacteria are only active in tumor cells and not in healthy human tissue. Our bacteria’s genome has been further transformed to express mCardinal red fluorescent protein, allowing any patient to use our PD made fluorescent reader device to physically see the tumor metastasis through the skin by a red glow. In order to effectively supplement the patient’s main cancer therapy, the bacteria have been designed to also release Trichosanthin, a more mild variation of the Ricin toxin, which kills tumor cells by deactivating their ribosomal function.
